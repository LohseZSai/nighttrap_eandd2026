\title{NightTrap: A Benchmark for Night Camera-Trap Review Pipelines}
\author{Anonymous submission}
\date{}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
Camera traps are widely used for wildlife monitoring, but their outputs still require substantial human review. In practice, review is not a single-image species-classification problem, but a multi-step workflow. Reviewers need to decide whether an image or event is empty, whether the visual evidence is usable, which species is present, how many animals are visible, and which events should enter the human-review queue. Night data makes this workflow more difficult because infrared imaging, low illumination, motion blur, overexposure, near-black frames, and occlusion can all reduce visual evidence. We introduce NightTrap, a benchmark for evaluating night camera-trap review pipelines. The task design is supported by a questionnaire targeting ecological researchers, protected-area staff, and long-term users of camera-trap data, rather than being directly derived from generic vision tasks. NightTrap is built from 68,187 night events across 2,902 camera sites and organizes review into five tasks: Image usability filtering, Empty-event filtering, Species classification, Count-bin classification, and Needs-review recommendation. Empty filtering, species classification, and counting are not claimed as new tasks. The contribution of NightTrap is to organize these established but scattered steps into a night review pipeline and to evaluate whether models can assist human review.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

% 相机陷阱是野生动物监测中的重要工具。它可以长期部署在野外，无需人工值守即可捕捉动物活动。然而，相机陷阱并没有消除人工工作量，而是把大量工作转移到了后端审核阶段。研究人员仍然需要过滤无效事件、判断图像是否可判读、识别动物、估计数量，并决定哪些事件应当更早交由人工查看。
Camera traps are an important tool for wildlife monitoring. They can be deployed in the field for long periods and capture animal activity without continuous human presence. However, camera traps do not remove human labor. They shift much of the work to downstream review, where researchers must filter invalid events, assess image usability, identify animals, estimate counts, and decide which events need human review.

% 夜间相机陷阱数据使这一流程更加困难。相比白天图像，夜间图像常常缺少颜色和纹理信息，并依赖红外或低照度成像。动物可能只呈现模糊轮廓；近距离动物可能被红外打白；远距离动物可能过暗而难以识别；运动中的动物也更容易模糊。因此，夜间审核的第一个问题往往不是“这是什么动物”，而是“这张图是否有足够视觉证据支持可靠判断”。
Night camera-trap data makes this workflow harder. Compared with daytime images, night images often lose color and texture information and rely on infrared or low-light imaging. Animals may appear only as weak silhouettes; nearby animals may be overexposed by infrared illumination; distant animals may be too dark to identify; and moving animals can be blurred. For this reason, the first question in night review is often not ``what species is this?'', but ``is there enough visual evidence to make a reliable judgment?''

Existing camera-trap benchmarks have studied animal presence, empty filtering, species classification, counting, and generalization to unseen camera sites \citep{norouzzadeh2017snapshot,schneider2018camera,choinski2021first,beery2019iwildcam,koh2021wilds}. These tasks are important, but they are often evaluated as isolated recognition tasks. Real night review is a pipeline: reviewers first decide whether evidence is usable, then whether the event is empty, then which species and count are present, and finally which events require human attention rather than being deprioritized for later review. In this small sample, respondents' difficulties align with a full review workflow rather than a single species-identification problem. When asked about the main difficulties in processing monitoring data, 83.33\% selected poor photo quality as a major issue, 58.33\% selected excessive data volume, 58.33\% selected difficulty in counting animals without repeated counting, and 50.00\% selected the lack of a systematic method for understanding animal behavior. We use these responses to motivate the task organization, not as large-scale user-study evidence.

More importantly, respondents did not want a model to fully replace experts. For the functions they rated highest, 54.55\% expected the tool to primarily reduce the burden of excessive data volume, and 27.3\% expected it to standardize team workflows. Motivated by these responses, NightTrap organizes night camera-trap review into five tasks: Image usability filtering, Empty-event filtering, Species classification, Count-bin classification, and Needs-review recommendation. These tasks correspond to recurring review steps in the questionnaire: judging low-quality visual evidence, filtering invalid or empty triggers, identifying species, estimating counts, and deciding which events require human review under limited review budgets. Empty filtering, species classification, and counting are not claimed as new tasks. The contribution of NightTrap is to organize these established but scattered steps into a night review pipeline and to evaluate whether models can assist human review.

% 本文实验也围绕这一需求展开：模型能否完成基础筛查和识别，并在有限人工审核预算下把更需要人工查看的事件排到前面。我们因此同时报告分类指标、排序指标和 safe auto-pass 指标，以区分“协助审核排序”和“安全减少人工工作量”这两种不同能力。
Our experiments follow the same need: can models perform basic filtering and recognition, and then recommend which events need human attention under limited review budgets? We therefore report classification metrics, review-score ranking metrics, and safe auto-pass metrics, so that review assistance through queue ordering can be distinguished from safe workload reduction.

% 总结起来，本文的主要贡献如下：
To summarize, our main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}

    \item We introduce \textbf{NightTrap}, a night camera-trap review benchmark built from 68,187 night events across 2,902 camera sites, designed to evaluate the full human review workflow rather than isolated species recognition.

    \item We organize night review into five linked tasks---image usability, empty-event filtering, species classification, count-bin classification, and needs-review recommendation---so that models can be evaluated at the same decision points used by human reviewers.

    \item We define a practical needs-review recommendation task with review-queue metrics and recall-constrained safe auto-pass analysis, distinguishing queue ordering from safe automatic pass-through.

    \item We evaluate closed-source references, open-source VLMs, and camera-trap baselines, revealing five actionable findings: long-tail species failures, the importance of image usability, limited safe auto-pass, shortcut risk from site context, and the boundary between detector screening and full review.

\end{enumerate}
\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Existing Camera-Trap Datasets and Benchmarks}

% 相机陷阱数据集。早期相机陷阱 benchmark 主要关注动物存在判断、物种识别、计数和跨相机泛化。Snapshot Serengeti 证明了深度学习可以在大规模相机陷阱图像上进行自动动物识别、计数和描述；Caltech Camera Traps、iWildCam 和 WILDS-iWildCam 进一步强调，相机陷阱数据存在显著的点位偏移和背景记忆问题，因此不能只依赖随机划分评估模型。
Camera-trap datasets. Existing camera-trap benchmarks mainly focus on animal presence, species recognition, counting, and cross-camera generalization. Snapshot Serengeti showed that deep learning can automatically identify, count, and describe wild animals in large-scale camera-trap images \citep{norouzzadeh2017snapshot}. Caltech Camera Traps, iWildCam, and WILDS-iWildCam further emphasize that camera-trap data contains strong site-level distribution shifts and background memorization risks, making random splits insufficient for evaluating real deployment performance \citep{beery2019iwildcam,koh2021wilds}. This follows a broader benchmark-design pattern in vision and language: useful benchmarks specify task definitions, splits, and metrics rather than only collecting images or prompts \citep{deng2009imagenet,lin2014coco,wang2019glue,rajpurkar2016squad,hu2020ogb}.

% 检测和分类。另一类工作将相机陷阱审核转化为检测或分类问题。检测模型可以过滤空图、定位动物并支持后续分类；分类模型可以识别常见物种并减少人工标注量。这些工作对生态监测非常重要，但任务通常以单图识别、检测或分类为核心，缺少对夜间审核流程中“图像是否可判读”“事件是否需要优先人工查看”等前后衔接环节的统一建模。
Detection and classification. Another line of work formulates camera-trap review as object detection or classification. Detection models can filter empty images, localize animals, and support downstream classification, while classification models can identify common species and reduce manual labeling effort \citep{schneider2018camera,choinski2021first,gomez2016towards}. These works are important for ecological monitoring, but they usually center on single-image detection or recognition. They do not jointly model the surrounding review steps, such as whether the image evidence is usable or whether an event should be moved earlier in a human review queue.

Detector-crop and retrieval baselines. Practical camera-trap systems often use detector crops before species classification: a detector proposes animal regions, and a downstream classifier or reviewer handles the cropped evidence \citep{beery2019pipeline,schneider2018camera}. Recent representation models such as CLIP and DINOv2 also make kNN retrieval or linear-probe baselines attractive, because they test whether frozen visual features already separate species before a task-specific VLM is introduced \citep{radford2021clip,oquab2023dinov2}. Supervised visual backbones such as ViT and ConvNeXt provide complementary task-specific baselines \citep{dosovitskiy2021vit,liu2022convnext}, while long-tail recognition work motivates reporting balanced metrics rather than only aggregate accuracy \citep{gupta2019lvis,vanhorn2018inaturalist,lin2017focalloss}. Knowledge-augmented zero-shot systems such as WildMatch push in a related direction by generating animal descriptions and matching them to external species knowledge \citep{fabian2023wildmatch}. These methods are complementary to NightTrap: they are important baselines for the species and count steps, while NightTrap evaluates the larger night-review workflow around those recognition decisions.



\subsection{Animal-Centric Vision and Multimodal Benchmarks}

% 动物视觉理解 benchmark。近年来的动物中心 benchmark 覆盖了更丰富的动物视觉理解任务。例如 Animal-Bench 评估多模态视频模型的动物中心理解能力，Animal Kingdom 关注动物行为理解，MammalNet 提供大规模哺乳动物识别和行为理解视频 benchmark。这些数据集拓展了动物视觉理解的范围，但它们通常不面向相机陷阱审核流程，也不将空图过滤、坏图过滤、数量分档和优先审核排序作为统一评估链条。
Animal-centric vision benchmarks. Recent animal-centric benchmarks cover a broader range of animal understanding tasks. Animal-Bench evaluates animal-centered video understanding for multimodal video models \citep{jing2024animalbench}; Animal Kingdom focuses on animal behavior understanding \citep{ng2022animalkingdom}; and MammalNet provides a large-scale video benchmark for mammal recognition and behavior understanding \citep{chen2023mammalnet}. These benchmarks broaden the scope of animal visual understanding, but they are not designed around camera-trap review workflows. In particular, they do not unify empty filtering, image usability filtering, count-bin classification, and needs-review recommendation as one evaluation pipeline.



\subsection{Camera-Trap Review Systems and Automation Methods}

% 自动审核系统。相机陷阱审核系统通常将检测器、分类器和人工复核结合起来，以减少空图和低价值图像带来的人工负担。典型流程包括先检测动物，再分类物种，最后将低置信度或高风险样本交给人工确认。这些系统说明实际部署并不追求模型完全替代专家，而是追求减少人工负担和提高审核效率。
Automated review systems. Camera-trap review systems often combine detectors, classifiers, and human verification to reduce the manual burden caused by empty or low-value images. A typical pipeline first detects animals, then classifies species, and finally sends low-confidence or high-risk samples to human reviewers \citep{beery2019pipeline}. These systems show that real deployment does not necessarily aim to replace experts fully; it aims to reduce review burden and improve review efficiency.

% 零样本和 VLM 方法。随着 VLM 和大模型的发展，研究者开始探索用零样本或少样本方式处理相机陷阱图像。此类方法具有灵活性，可以通过自然语言 prompt 输出物种、数量、行为或不确定性描述，但也容易受到视觉证据不足、长尾物种、红外图像分布偏移和上下文捷径的影响。
Zero-shot and VLM-based methods. With the development of VLMs and large language models, researchers have begun to explore zero-shot or few-shot camera-trap image categorization \citep{vyskocil2024zeroshot}. General VLM systems show that language-conditioned visual reasoning can be transferred across tasks \citep{radford2021clip,alayrac2022flamingo,li2023blip2}, but benchmark design still has to separate visual evidence from answer-revealing context. These methods are flexible because they can use natural language prompts to produce species labels, counts, behavior descriptions, or uncertainty estimates. However, they are also vulnerable to insufficient visual evidence, long-tailed species, infrared appearance shifts, and context shortcuts.

Table~\ref{tab:related-work-matrix} gives a compact coverage comparison. The main difference is that NightTrap is not only a species-recognition benchmark: it evaluates the surrounding night-review steps that determine whether an event is usable, safely empty, countable, and worth sending to a human reviewer.
\section{NightTrap Benchmark}

NightTrap is a benchmark for evaluating whether models can support a night camera-trap review workflow. The benchmark is built around the order in which a human reviewer would process a night event: first decide whether the evidence is usable, then reject safe empty triggers, then identify the animal and estimate count, and finally decide whether the event needs human review. Figure~\ref{fig:nighttrap_benchmark_overview} summarizes how source data, questionnaire-motivated design constraints, event-level task construction, and needs-review routing checks fit together. This section describes how the data is constructed, how the five tasks are defined, and how we control label leakage and annotation ambiguity.

An \textbf{event} is a trigger burst or grouped camera-trap record from one camera site. It may contain one or more frames captured within the same trigger sequence. NightTrap represents each event with up to three image slots---first, middle, and last---because this approximates how reviewers inspect a short burst. When an event has fewer distinct frames, slots may repeat; in the frozen catalog, 64.1\% of events have identical image paths in all three slots. We therefore treat the input as event-level review evidence rather than as a single image, a full video, or a temporal-reasoning task.

\subsection{Data construction pipeline}

\paragraph{Data sources and frozen event catalog.}
NightTrap is built from four established camera-trap sources: Caltech Camera Traps, Snapshot Serengeti, WCS Camera Traps, and Idaho Camera Traps. We first convert the raw assets into a normalized relational inventory. The inventory contains an event table, a frame table, and lookup tables for source dataset, camera site, species, time, sensor mode, and visibility status. This structure follows dataset-documentation recommendations that emphasize data provenance, intended use, and known limitations \citep{gebru2021datasheets,bender2018datastatements,mitchell2019modelcards}. It is used only for construction and auditing; models never see database internals. The released benchmark uses a frozen night-event catalog derived from this inventory after night filtering, path validation, event deduplication, and fixed image-slot construction. The final catalog contains \textbf{68,187 night events}, \textbf{115,617 frame records}, \textbf{2,902 camera sites}, and \textbf{283 species}. We define a camera site as the pair of source dataset and source location identifier, because location identifiers are not globally unique across datasets.

\paragraph{Night filtering and event representation.}
We use the catalog field \texttt{time\_of\_day\_bucket = night} to select nocturnal events. Each event keeps up to three fixed image slots: first, middle, and last. This matches how reviewers inspect a trigger burst, but it should not be over-interpreted as uniformly rich temporal evidence. In the frozen catalog, \textbf{64.1\%} of events have identical image paths in all three slots, while \textbf{32.7\%} have three unique image paths. This is why the benchmark evaluates event-level review evidence rather than video understanding.

\paragraph{Task-pool construction.}
Each task is built from the frozen event catalog but uses the most reliable label source for that decision. \textbf{Empty filtering} is built from a MegaDetector-v5a-assisted empty-event asset pool and then rejoined to the frozen catalog. We use two labels: \texttt{empty\_false\_trigger} and \texttt{nonempty\_or\_uncertain}. The latter intentionally includes uncertain animal cases, because the task is safe empty rejection rather than animal detection at all costs. \textbf{Species classification} uses closed-set species labels from night events, with dev/test labels required to appear in the training label set. \textbf{Count-bin classification} uses Snapshot Serengeti night events with count labels and maps exact counts into \texttt{1}, \texttt{2}, \texttt{3--5}, and \texttt{6+}. \textbf{Image usability filtering} is a hard evidence-quality set built from candidate examples of sufficient and insufficient visual evidence. \textbf{Needs-review recommendation} is built only from image-usable animal events, so low-quality images are handled upstream rather than being mixed into the human-review decision.

\paragraph{Splits and leakage control.}
All A/B/C task splits are site-disjoint and audited for event, site, and image overlap. Needs-review recommendation uses stricter controls because site context is part of the input. For each target event, the site reference history is built only from earlier events at the same site. The target event itself and future events are excluded. Missing reference history is not backfilled from the full catalog. This prevents the benchmark from turning site context into a hidden answer key.

\subsection{Review workflow tasks}

NightTrap reports five evaluation tasks. Several of these tasks have precedent in prior camera-trap work; our contribution is to connect them as one reproducible night review workflow and to expose where current models fail under night imaging constraints. For classification tasks, we use Accuracy as the overall fraction of correct predictions and macro-averaged F1 (Macro-F1) to measure performance under class imbalance, because each class contributes equally regardless of frequency.

\paragraph{Image usability filtering.}
\textbf{Objective.} This task asks whether the visual evidence is sufficient for downstream review. It is not an animal-presence task: a usable image may be empty, and an unusable image may contain an animal that is near-black, severely blurred, occluded, or overexposed.
\textbf{Construction.} We build a hard evidence-quality set from candidate examples of sufficient and insufficient visual evidence. Simple image-quality features, such as brightness, saturation, local overexposure, contrast, and sharpness, are used only to surface candidate failures and boundary cases. Final labels are assigned at the review-decision level rather than copied from these screening rules.
\textbf{Metric.} We report Accuracy and Macro-F1. Macro-F1 is important because the benchmark must measure both usable and unusable evidence, rather than rewarding a model for predicting only the majority label.

\paragraph{Empty-event filtering.}
\textbf{Objective.} This task asks whether an event is a safe empty trigger: a visually judgeable event in which no valid animal subject is visible. It is not meant to find every possible animal; uncertain animal-like cases are treated conservatively.
\textbf{Construction.} We start from a MegaDetector-v5a-assisted empty-event asset pool, match candidates back to the frozen night catalog, and use two labels: \texttt{empty\_false\_trigger} and \texttt{nonempty\_or\_uncertain}. The second label intentionally includes ambiguous cases because the intended operation is safe empty rejection rather than aggressive data deletion.
\textbf{Metric.} We report Accuracy and Macro-F1 in the main table, with additional empty-recall details in the appendix. Macro-F1 prevents the large nonempty-or-uncertain class from hiding failures on the empty class.

\paragraph{Species classification.}
\textbf{Objective.} This task evaluates closed-set recognition of the main visible species in a night event under infrared, low-light, blur, and occlusion artifacts.
\textbf{Construction.} We select night events with available species labels and valid image paths, restrict the task to labels with enough support for evaluation, and require dev/test species to appear in the training split. The released task contains 55 species and uses site-disjoint splits.
\textbf{Metric.} We report Accuracy and Macro-F1. Accuracy shows the overall fraction of correct predictions, while Macro-F1 is the primary signal for long-tailed ecological labels because rare species should not disappear behind common-species accuracy.

\paragraph{Count-bin classification.}
\textbf{Objective.} This task estimates coarse visible-animal count ranges rather than exact counts. The label space is \texttt{1}, \texttt{2}, \texttt{3--5}, and \texttt{6+}.
\textbf{Construction.} We use Snapshot Serengeti night events with count labels, because this source provides the most reliable count supervision in the catalog. Exact counts are mapped into bins to match practical review needs.
\textbf{Metric.} We report Accuracy and Macro-F1. Coarse bins are intentional: night events often contain repeated frames, partial bodies, occlusion, and overlapping individuals, making exact individual counts less stable than practical count ranges.

\paragraph{Needs-review recommendation.}
\textbf{Objective.} This task asks whether an image-usable animal event should enter the human review queue. The main benchmark target is binary: a \texttt{routine} event maps to \texttt{needs\_review}=0, and a \texttt{review} event maps to \texttt{needs\_review}=1.
\textbf{Construction.} We first remove events that belong to image-usability filtering, so the task is not driven by low-quality evidence. We then assign benchmark-defined binary labels from visible event evidence and allowed site-reference context. The 983-event hardset was constructed to support controlled image/context comparisons and queue-ordering checks, while the 6000-event routine-stress set is used as the main realistic review-queue setting because its needs-review base rate is 30\%.
\textbf{Metric.} Models output both a binary decision and a continuous review score. We evaluate binary decisions with Accuracy and Macro-F1, and we evaluate review scores with P@K, Enrichment@K, AUROC/AUPRC where frozen continuous scores exist, and safe auto-pass under high needs-review recall. These metrics separate queue ordering from safe automatic pass-through.

\paragraph{Input settings for needs-review recommendation.}
Researchers do not review camera-trap events in a vacuum. They often know the site, season, and animals historically seen at that location. NightTrap simulates this lightweight reviewer knowledge while excluding fields that would reveal the answer, such as the current species label, novelty flag, rarity label, future events, or the gold decision. We use the same terminology for all needs-review experiments. \textbf{Image-only} means that the model sees only the current event images. \textbf{Context-lite-only} means that the model sees only allowed site-history context, including season and species previously observed at the same site, but no target-event images. \textbf{Full context-lite} means that the model sees both the current event images and the same allowed site-history context. The three settings are not three different tasks; they are controlled input views for testing whether a method uses current visual evidence, historical site priors, or both.

\paragraph{Strict hardset and routine-stress set.}
NightTrap uses three needs-review evaluation sets for different purposes. The \textbf{6000-event routine-stress set} is the main operational setting: it contains 4200 routine cases and 1800 review cases, so the needs-review base rate is 30\%. This setting is used for the headline queue result because it reflects a routine-heavy review queue where most image-usable animal events do not require immediate human review. The \textbf{983-event hardset} supports controlled image-only, context-lite-only, and full context-lite comparisons. After the binary collapse, it contains 140 routine cases and 843 needs-review positives, or 85.8\% positives. This high positive rate is intentional: the hardset is not a deployment base-rate estimate, but a stress test for input settings and queue ordering. The \textbf{913-event CLIP/context split} is a derived frozen subset used by supervised ranker checks, because the CLIP scripts retain only rows with available frozen image embeddings and valid context-lite features. We therefore do not compare 6000, 983, and 913 as if they were interchangeable leaderboards.

The task table reports the 6000-event routine-stress set for Needs-review because that is the main review-queue setting. The 983-event hardset is retained for controlled input and queue-ordering checks in the appendix.

\subsection{Distribution and annotation reliability}

\paragraph{Long-tail and source heterogeneity.}
NightTrap is not balanced to look clean. It preserves the heterogeneity of night camera-trap data. Source datasets differ in site density, species composition, geography, label granularity, and night imaging mode. WCS contributes most sites, while Caltech Camera Traps and Snapshot Serengeti contribute large event volumes. Species and sites are both long-tailed: the median species has 12 night events, and the median site has 5 night events. This is why we report Macro-F1, per-task checks, and ranking metrics rather than relying only on overall accuracy.

\paragraph{Image-usability reliability.}
We use inter-annotator agreement (IAA) to check whether the usability boundary is reliable. The audit deliberately contains clear usable images, clear unusable images, and ambiguous boundary cases. As expected, agreement is only moderate for the three-way usable/unusable/uncertain labeling, because annotators may reasonably disagree on boundary cases. However, the operational decision used by the benchmark is binary: whether the image can be trusted for downstream review. Across 298 valid paired annotations, three-way agreement is 72.5\% with Cohen's $\kappa=0.441$. Excluding uncertain cases yields 98.5\% agreement with $\kappa=0.911$, and grouping uncertain with unsafe or boundary evidence yields 86.9\% agreement with $\kappa=0.703$. This means NightTrap does not claim perfect agreement on every boundary image; it uses IAA to show that the safety boundary between usable evidence and evidence that should not be trusted is reliable.

\paragraph{Needs-review label scope.}
We do not claim that \texttt{review} is an expert ecological-review label. A complete ecological agreement study would require domain experts and a separate annotation setup. Instead, NightTrap treats \texttt{needs\_review} as a benchmark-defined human-review recommendation label and audits how sampled labels behave under relabeling with source species and count information. In a 100-event label-aware relabeling audit, the benchmark routine/review label was hidden while source species and count labels were shown; 98 rows were binary-evaluable and 2 were marked uncertain. Agreement was 54.1\% with Cohen's $\kappa=0.082$, with 49.0\% routine-label agreement and 59.2\% review-label agreement. This low agreement reinforces that needs-review is a subjective review-assistance target rather than expert ecological ground truth. The primary practical question remains the binary distinction between routine cases and events that need human review. This conservative framing is intentional: the task is designed to evaluate review assistance, not to replace ecological judgment.

\paragraph{What the benchmark measures.}
The resulting benchmark measures three practical questions. First, can a model safely separate empty triggers from events that should not be discarded? Second, can it make semantic judgments about species and count under night imaging constraints? Third, can it enrich needs-review events near the top of a queue using the current event and allowed site history, rather than answer-revealing fields? These are narrower than full ecological intelligence, but they are directly aligned with the workload bottlenecks of night camera-trap review.




\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:experiments_results}

\subsection{Experimental setup}

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics.}
NightTrap evaluates four review-workflow classification tasks and one human-review recommendation task. Image usability, Empty-event, Species, and Count-bin report Accuracy and Macro-F1, with Macro-F1 as the primary metric because night camera-trap labels are imbalanced and long-tailed \citep{lin2017focalloss,gupta2019lvis}. Needs-review recommendation uses review-queue operating metrics because the practical question is not only whether a single event receives the correct binary label, but whether a limited review budget sees the right events early. P@K is the fraction of needs-review events among the top K scored events. Enrichment@K is P@K divided by the positive base rate. NDCG follows the standard graded-ranking view of top-ranked utility \citep{jarvelin2002ndcg}, while AUROC and AUPRC support threshold-independent binary scoring under class imbalance \citep{davis2006prroc}. Safe auto-pass is a recall-constrained operating point: for a target needs-review recall $r$, it reports the largest fraction of events that can be automatically passed while maintaining recall at least $r$, following the selective-prediction principle that abstention and automation require their own operating analysis \citep{geifman2017selective}. These review-queue metrics complement Accuracy and Macro-F1; they are not proposed as replacement general-purpose metrics. Bootstrap intervals are reported only for result files that already contain them.

\paragraph{Models.}
We organize models by their role in the benchmark rather than by a single leaderboard. Closed-source VLMs are retained as reference rows, not reproducibility anchors. Open-source VLMs, including Qwen2-VL-2B and Qwen3-VL-8B, provide auditable multimodal baselines under the image-only, context-lite-only, or full context-lite input views defined above. NVILA-8B is reported separately as a tuned multimodal comparison: the checked local checkpoint is an NVILA-family VLM adapted from a pretrained NVILA/NVILA-Lite model on AnimalLLaMA data with a frozen SigLIP/ViT-style vision tower. It is not counted as a task-specific fine-tuned ViT or ConvNeXt classifier trained on NightTrap splits. Standard camera-trap and representation baselines include majority or random scoring, CLIP and DINOv2 linear probes, ViT-B/16 and ConvNeXt-Tiny task-specific supervised vision fine-tunes for the four classification tasks, detector-crop CLIP and DINOv2 species baselines with full-frame fallback, CLIP/context-lite logistic rankers, XGBoost and LightGBM needs-review ranker variants, rule-only checks, context-lite-only checks, and MegaDetector thresholding for the empty-event task \citep{radford2021clip,oquab2023dinov2,dosovitskiy2021vit,liu2022convnext,chen2016xgboost,ke2017lightgbm}. No dedicated needs-review fine-tuned vision baseline is included because needs-review is evaluated as a review-queue ranking task with context-lite inputs; supervised needs-review is covered by the CLIP/context-lite logistic ranker and ranker variants on the frozen 913-event split. The appendix gives full run details; the main text keeps only rows with frozen metrics that support the five benchmark questions.

\subsection{Ablations and shortcut checks}

Appendix Table~\ref{tab:ablation_diagnostics} condenses the v0.8 ablations and shortcut checks. Shuffled-context, context-lite-only, and site-frequency-only checks test whether models bind the right image to the right site history rather than exploiting priors. The event-representation ablation also shows that adding more image slots does not automatically add useful evidence: first-frame-only and three-slot mean pooling both reach P@50 of 40.00 on the CLIP needs-review split.

Robustness checks show that imaging mode and source composition are part of the benchmark rather than nuisance metadata. Night-IR events reduce needs-review P@50 for Qwen3-VL-8B relative to night-color events, and count-bin evaluation is source-constrained because reliable count labels come from Snapshot Serengeti. The MegaDetector-assisted empty pool remains useful infrastructure, but its 100-item human audit is treated only as risk quantification, not detector-independent proof.

\section{Conclusion}

% NightTrap 评估夜间相机陷阱审核流程，包括五个任务：Image usability filtering、Empty-event filtering、Species classification、Count-bin classification 和 Needs-review recommendation。它将不可判读图像与空图分开，并且只在图像可判读的动物事件上评估人工审核推荐。
We introduced NightTrap, a benchmark for evaluating night camera-trap review pipelines. Unlike species-only recognition benchmarks, NightTrap evaluates review as a linked workflow: models judge usable evidence, filter empty events, classify species, estimate count bins, and prioritize image-usable animal events for human review. This mirrors how night data is processed: an event may first be unusable, then empty, and only after those checks do species, count, and needs-review decisions become meaningful.

Our evaluation shows that current VLMs can help order human review queues, but cannot yet decide safely which events never need review. Queue-front enrichment improves, while safe automatic pass-through remains small at high needs-review recall. Species accuracy can be much higher than Macro-F1, exposing long-tail failures behind strong aggregate accuracy. Context helps, but site history can resemble visual reasoning unless image-only, context-lite-only, shuffled-context, and site-frequency checks are reported together. NightTrap therefore frames near-term camera-trap AI as reviewer assistance: surfacing likely important events earlier, flagging weak evidence, and supporting expert confirmation rather than replacing it.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
NightTrap has several limitations. The needs-review label is an operational review label rather than expert ecological ground truth; the low-agreement label-aware relabeling audit reinforces that this is a subjective review-assistance target, not a validation claim \citep{northcutt2021labelerrors}. The 6000-event routine-stress set is the main queue benchmark, while the 983-event hardset is a review-heavy input and queue-ordering check. Some VLM scores use frozen multiple-choice probabilities rather than a calibrated binary head, so they should not be read as deployment probabilities \citep{guo2017calibration}. NightTrap also focuses on night events and does not yet cover daytime review, behavior annotation, open-ended ecological reporting, or causal reasoning. Future work can extend NightTrap toward agentic review workflows: an agent could screen usability and empty triggers, retrieve site-history context, compare visually similar prior events, request human confirmation only for uncertain or high-value cases, and maintain an auditable review log to further reduce manual camera-trap review while keeping experts in the loop.

\subsection{LLM and VLM Usage Declaration}

VLMs are core experimental subjects in NightTrap, not merely writing tools. Prompt templates and answer schemas are fixed for each task and should be released with the artifact package. Open-source VLM rows, especially Qwen2-VL and Qwen3-VL, are the main model evidence. NVILA is a tuned multimodal comparison and should not be used to close the supervised classifier gap. GPT reference rows are closed-source exploratory references and are not the main reproducibility anchor.

LLM assistance may have been used for editing, drafting, and checklist cleanup. It was not used to generate benchmark labels or experimental results unless explicitly stated elsewhere in the paper. Reported numbers come from frozen scripts, prediction files, and table fragments.

\subsection{Broader Impacts and Safeguards}

NightTrap may reduce the burden of reviewing night camera-trap data and help researchers decide which events should be checked first. The safe auto-pass analysis also exposes an important boundary: models can improve review ordering, but current systems should not be trusted to automatically discard many routine cases under high needs-review recall constraints.

The main risks are over-reliance on model outputs, missing events that need review, leakage of rare-species or sensitive site information, and bias introduced by detector-assisted empty-event pools. The release plan mitigates these risks through human-in-the-loop framing, high-recall safe auto-pass reporting, site/location anonymization, license-gated raw-image access, no redistribution of restricted raw images, and a stratified manual-review manifest plus a 100-item human audit for the MegaDetector-assisted empty pool.

\subsection{Questionnaire and Human-Participant Notes}

The questionnaire collected high-level role and usage-preference statistics to motivate task design. It did not collect sensitive personal attributes and was not used to generate labels or experimental results. We do not claim IRB approval. The questionnaire is treated as low-risk, non-interventional aggregate motivation material rather than as a label-generation or outcome-measurement study.
